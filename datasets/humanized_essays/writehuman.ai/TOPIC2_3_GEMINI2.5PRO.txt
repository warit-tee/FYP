Safety comes first before starting. The most important step is to ensure that the fan is unplugged to eliminate any risk of electric shock. Once it is inert gather the supplies. The fan repair can only be successful if the required repairs are done and the right tools are included. The tools include a set of Phillips and flat-head screwdrivers, pliers, a soft cloth, a small brush, and light lubricating oil, like 3-in-1 oil. Letting these tools be organized will promote a smoother flowing procedure.

Once safety has been secured and all the requisite tools collected, the next step is diagnosis. At this stage, one is required to systematically observe the different potential hints that the device presents before taking the step of disassembling it. Start by visually inspecting the power cord and check for any signs of fraying or other forms of damage. Then, try to spin the fan blades by hand. If they spin freely, then the problem is most likely electrical. If the blades are stiff or completely frozen, then the problem is almost certainly a mechanical one, specifically a jam within the motor's rotating assembly. A low humming noise before failure often suggests the motor is receiving power but is unable to turn, this is often the result of a seized motor shaft. This primary assessment is helpful to the repair and serves to narrow down potential causes.

After a preliminary diagnosis is made, you may start a careful, listening disassembly. Try to get to the motor without damaging anything. Start by removing the protective grills covering the fan blades. These are usually clipped or screwed in place. Store all the parts you remove in a container so you do not misplace anything. After removing the front grill, you may turn your attention to the fan blade assembly. To remove the blades, you must first remove a plastic cap which is reverse-threaded, making it necessary for you to turn it clockwise to remove the cap. The blades should then slide off the shaft easily. After removing the blades, unscrew any remaining fasteners that hold the plastic motor casing together. This will enable you to expose the motor.

Most fan failures do not occur from severe electrical burnout, but rather from a straightforward mechanical problem: the gathering of dust, hair, and dirt. Clogged ventilation, as well as the passage of time, leads to dirt and other particles working their way into the gaps around the motor shaft. This buildup becomes so thick and fused with the original grease that it becomes a solid mass. The heavy sludge increases the friction to the point that the motor cannot move, and the motor burns out. The solution is a complete and superb cleaning. A small brush and a dry cloth are adequate to get all the dust from the motor casing and vents. The shaft debris that covers the front and back of the motor and enters the bearings and needs to be extracted as well.

You can then proceed to the lubrication step. For this step, you will want to use light machine oil. Put a few drops on the shaft where the shaft enters the motor casing on the front and back. Oil the shaft, and then turn it with your fingers to help the oil penetrate the bearings. You can feel the resistance drop, and the shaft will start to move more freely. Keep adding a drop or two until the shaft turns easily. This step resolves the most common cause of a "dead" fan.

Once the fan has been cleaned and the components lubricated, the fan is ready to be put back together. This is just the reverse of the steps taken to take the fan apart, so first, take the motor housing and secure it in place, making sure all the wires are neatly tucked away. Then, attach the rear grill again. Then, the fan blades on the motor shaft, and secure them with the spinner cap, turning it counter-clockwise again to tighten it. Then, clip the front grill in place. Finally, before turning on the fan, give the fan blades a spin. Once you put the fan in, it will work and give a breeze.
